## 2) A concrete “story arc” rewrite plan (Act I → Act II → Act III → Evidence)

### Act I (Problem): what *really* goes wrong in deployed agents

Write this like a security paper, not like an RFC.

Use **one running incident** throughout the paper, e.g.:

Then state the key observation:

* **“Reasoning space” is compromise-prone; the only reliable place to enforce is “execution space”.**
* However, the moment you centralize execution checks, **you create a privacy sink** (the control plane sees all prompts/tools/recipients/tokens).

This lines up perfectly with your draft’s “control-plane privacy paradox.”

### Act II (Why existing systems fail): two structural impossibilities

This is where you “hard-check” Faramesh and everything else.

You want two crisp impossibility-style claims (not necessarily formal impossibility, but structural):

1. **Without a non-bypassable executor-side check, runtime defenses can be bypassed** once the runtime is compromised.
2. **With a centralized plaintext policy engine, the defense becomes a privacy sink** (privacy paradox).

Faramesh is strong on (1) but does not address (2) (and explicitly focuses on execution-time authorization). Faramesh introduces AAB/CAR/deterministic authorization/fail-closed/replayability, but there is no privacy-preserving control-plane mechanism (no PIR/MPC/privacy discussion in the arXiv HTML). ([arXiv][1])

### Act III (Your key idea): “same boundary, but with two missing invariants”

Then introduce SecureClaw as:

* **An AAB-style boundary** (PREVIEW→COMMIT) where the executor only executes after verifying *cryptographically bound* evidence. (This is your NBE line.)
* Plus **privacy-preserving policy outsourcing** (SAP) that allows centralized enforcement *without* revealing decision inputs to any single policy server.
* Plus **handle-first secrets** so the runtime never sees plaintext secrets by default, cutting off prompt-injection exfil at the root.

A reviewer-friendly mapping is:

> **SecureClaw adopts the AAB philosophy, but shows it is insufficient for enterprise deployment unless the control plane is privacy-preserving and the runtime is plaintext-blind.**

A small table with rows like:

## 7) The one utility issue you must address before submission

Two numbers are a reviewer magnet:

* In your main table: **Benign allow = 80%** for SecureClaw (504/2520 benign denied).
* In your parity lane: benign scenario OR metric is much lower for SecureClaw than plain/DRIFT/IPIGuard (suggesting significant utility loss depending on how that benchmark defines “benign”).

Even if your security is perfect, **80% benign allow will often be viewed as “too expensive” unless you explain it extremely clearly.**

### How to handle this in a reviewer-proof way

1. **Define utility precisely** (is “REQUIRE_CONFIRM” counted as allowed or denied?).
   Your protocol explicitly distinguishes `allow_pre` and `need_confirm` and requires confirmation for commit when `need_confirm=1`.
2. Add two utility metrics:

   * **Auto-confirm utility** (upper bound, “if user confirms legitimate actions”)
   * **No-confirm utility** (lower bound, “if no human ever confirms”)
3. Provide a short qualitative breakdown:

   * “X% benign denies are due to policy strictness (expected)”
   * “Y% are due to capability mismatch / harness config”
   * “Z% are false positives we fix by …”

This reframes “80% benign allow” from “system unusable” into “policy choice + confirmation UX.”
Without that separation, reviewers will assume the system is brittle.
